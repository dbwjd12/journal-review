Mental health before and during the COVID-19 pandemic: a longitudinal probability sample survey of the UK population

Backgrounds

Methods

Study design and participants

  • UKHLS : The UK Household Longitudinal Study (UKHLS; Understanding Society) is an ongoing panel survey of more than 40000 households that began in 2009.

  • From April 23 to April 30, 2020, members of households who participated in either of the two most recent UKHLS data collections (Waves 8 or 9), who were older than age 16 years, were invited to complete the first wave of the COVID-19 web survey.

  • Both the UKHLS main survey waves and the COVID-19 web survey included the 12-item General Health Questionnaire (GHQ-12), which was used as the primary outcome measure.

  • Repeated cross-sectional analysis:
    UKHLS Waves 6–9 participants with available GHQ-12 data were included to examine overall temporal trends.

  • Fixed-effects analysis:
    Participants from Waves 8 or 9 who completed the COVID-19 survey and had prior GHQ-12 measures were included to evaluate within-person changes.

Outcomes

GHQ-12 Scoring

  • Mean score reflects average symptom burden; threshold reflects clinical distress prevalence.

  • Fixed-effects analyses used the mean GHQ-12 score (continuous outcome).

Mean symptoms score(0-36)

  • 0 = not at all

  • 1 = no more than usual

  • 2 = rather more than usual

  • 3 = much more than usual

Item scores summed across 12 questions

Binary threshold score (0–12):

Responses scored as:

  • 0 = not at all / no more than usual

  • 1 = rather more than usual / much more than usual

Scores summed across items

Clinically significant distress defined as ≥4

Statistical analysis

Repeated cross-sectional data

  • Descriptive summaries of GHQ-12 across survey waves

    • By survey wave (time)

    • By key sociodemographic characteristics, including:

      • Sex

      • Age group

      • Ethnicity

      • Household income

      • Other predefined subgroups

  • Mean scores and threshold prevalence reported (Tables 1–3)

  • Used to describe population-level trends, not individual change

Fixed-Effects Models

  • Analysis restricted to participants with linked pre-pandemic and COVID-19 GHQ-12 data

  • Fixed-effects linear regression :

    • ​A regression approach that estimates effects using within-person variation over time

    • Each individual serves as their own control

  • Model specification

    • Included:

      • Individual fixed effects

      • Calendar time, modelled as:

        • Linear term (time)

        • Quadratic term (time²)

      • COVID-19 period indicator

    • COVID-19 coefficient interpreted as:

      • The deviation from expected GHQ-12 scores based on pre-pandemic trends

  • Heterogeneity analysis

    • Tested interactions between:

      • COVID-19 indicator × predefined subgroups

    • Subgroups tested individually

    • Interactions with p < 0.05 included in a final adjusted model

    • Adjusted estimates interpreted as independent subgroup-specific changes, accounting for overlapping characteristics

Outcome

cross-sectional data

  • Mean GHQ-12 and distress prevalence increased over time

  • Larger symptom burden observed among women, younger adults, ethnic minorities, and low-income groups

  • Apparent widening of inequalities during the COVID-19 period

Fixed-effects analysis

Overall, GHQ-12 scores during the COVID-19 period were 0.48 points higher (95% CI 0.07–0.90) than would have been expected had pre-pandemic trends continued.

This indicates a population-wide deterioration in mental health beyond existing temporal trends.

\[ Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \,\text{COVID}_{it} + \beta_2 t + \beta_3 t^2 + \varepsilon_{it}, \]

The estimated coefficient

\[ \hat{\beta}_1 = 0.48 \]

represents the average deviation of observed GHQ-12 scores during the COVID-19 period from values expected based on pre-pandemic trends.

Subgroup Heterogeneity

  • To assess heterogeneity in pandemic-associated change, interactions between the COVID-19 indicator and predefined subgroups were fitted.

  • Each subgroup was first examined separately by fitting a fixed-effects linear regression model that included a single interaction term between the COVID-19 indicator and the subgroup variable.

  • Only subgroups for which the interaction term was statistically significant (two-sided ( p < 0.05 )) in these unadjusted models were retained for inclusion in the final multivariable model.

Women vs Men

Women experienced a substantially larger increase in GHQ-12 scores during the COVID-19 period compared with men.

\[ Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \text{COVID}_{it} + \gamma_1 (\text{COVID}_{it} \times \text{Female}_i) + \beta_2 t + \beta_3 t^2 + \varepsilon_{it}. \]

  • For men (reference group):

\[ \Delta_{\text{Men}} = \beta_1 \]

  • For women :

\[ \Delta_{\text{Women}} = \beta_1 + \gamma_1 \]

Age Groups

Younger adults showed larger within-person increases in GHQ-12 scores during the COVID-19 period compared with older age groups.

For a categorical age variable with reference group ( r ), the model can be written as:

\[ Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \text{COVID}_{it} + \sum_{k \neq r} \gamma_k (\text{COVID}_{it} \times \mathbb{1}\{\text{Age}_i = k\}) + \beta_2 t + \beta_3 t^2 + \varepsilon_{it}. \]

  • COVID-related change for the reference age group:

\[ \Delta_r = \beta_1 \]

  • COVID-related change for age group ( k ):

\[ \Delta_k = \beta_1 + \gamma_k \]

Discussion

  • COVID-19 was associated with a population-wide deterioration in mental health beyond pre-existing trends.

  • Apparent inequalities in cross-sectional analyses largely reflect baseline differences rather than differential within-person change.

  • Fixed-effects analyses identify women and younger adults experiencing greater deterioration.

limitations

  • Fixed-effects models cannot account for time-varying unmeasured confounders.

  • Analyses focused on the early phase of the pandemic and may not capture longer-term effects.
